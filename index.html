Is the movie or book better?

“THE BOOK WAS BETTER THAN THE MOVIE” – It’s a phrase we hear way too often, often uttered by diehard fans of books or series of books that are then disappointed by their big-screen counterpart. In recent times, studios have taken a backseat to writing new stories and have allowed writers to take the spotlight, using previously-written novels and stories as basis for motion pictures. But is this always the smart choice? As movies have fanbases, so do novels. And some of these fans will go to great lengths to defend their favorites. Sometimes movie adaptations will leave book fans saying the aforementioned phrase: “the book was better than the movie. The Data Dugongs have decided to put this phrase to the test, and compare the Goodreads ratings of books to the IMDB ratings of their movie counterparts.

We first used two Goodreads datasets in order to narrow down which book/film combinations we will look at. The lists together contain 2,511 books, however, when repeating offenders appeared, we narrowed the lists down to ____ entries.

The first list is literally titled “The Book was Better than the Movie.” In this dataset, a list of 1,497 books are ranked, from “greatest book in comparison to worst movie” to “worst book in comparison to greatest movie.”

The second list is the opposite of the claim, and is titled as such: “The Movie was Better than the Book.” 1,014 entries were listed, and they were ranked on movie upvotes.

The ratings of each entry's subsequent movie was also taken from IMDB, and intergrated into some of our graphs.

Social Media Polling

The Data Dugongs first wanted to see how the famous phrase held up against real data. In general, is the book REALLY better than the movie? We took to social media to find out.

Social media, as of recent, has become an amazing medium for polling. Sites such as Facebook and Twitter have incorporated polling into their everyday status updates for functionality purposes, making it easy for users to generate and ask their fanbases questions. We used this to our advantage and, using Instagram and Twitter, polled our personal followers to see how books held up against movies.

After the polling window closed, we had just shy of 100 users, in total, respond to our polls (65 on Instagram, 31 on Twitter. As seen above, books won out over their movie counterparts. While this was only a general survey, it seems the peers we surround ourselves with would much rather read a novel than watch a movie. But let's look bigger...
